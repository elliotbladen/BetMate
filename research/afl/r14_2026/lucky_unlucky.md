# AFL Round 14 2026 — Lucky vs Unlucky Teams

**Methodology:** Compare final scoreboard margin vs underlying statistical dominance. Key metrics: inside 50s differential, clearance differential, contested possessions, score-from-marks vs rushed behinds, accuracy (goals:behinds ratio), garbage-time distortion.

---

## UNLUCKY TEAMS (performed well, scoreboard didn't reflect it)

---

### PORT ADELAIDE — Unlucky Losers (lost by 3, led by 9 at 3QT)
**Result:** Lost 90-93 to Sydney
**Why unlucky:** Port led by nine points at three-quarter time and had controlled significant portions of the match. The Power's first half was statistically strong but criminally inaccurate: **seven behinds in the first half** while scoring only 3.6 at quarter-time — a 1-in-3 goal conversion rate. Port kicked the opening three goals of Q2 to blow the game open to a 20-point lead but then wasted a series of set shots.

**Scoreboard vs reality:**
- Port's shot efficiency was devastatingly poor: finished 13.12 (90) while kicking 25 total scoring shots vs Sydney's 23 total scoring shots (14.9, 93). Port had MORE scoring shots but lost.
- Port scored 7 behinds in Q2 alone — had those converted at league average (52%), Port leads by ~25 points at half-time, not 7.
- Sydney's comeback in Q4 (five goals to three) was legitimate — but Port gave it away in Q1-Q2 via inaccuracy.

**Conclusion:** Port should have won by 15-20 points. Their inside-50 output and territorial control was superior but catastrophic goalkicking handed Sydney the four points. This is a **genuine unlucky loss**, not a performance issue.

**Market context:** Port were $2.86 outsiders. Market was right directionally (Sydney favoured) but the game narrative was all Port until the final term.

---

### WEST COAST EAGLES — Unlucky Losers (lost by 1 point after leading fight-back)
**Result:** Lost 73-74 to North Melbourne
**Why unlucky:** West Coast trailed by 20 points early in Q4 and kicked the last three goals to take the lead... then Waterman hit the post from 30m out with 45 seconds remaining. From a -20 position, kicking three unanswered goals to give yourself a lead with a minute left is a performance of genuine quality — the one-point margin completely masks the final quarter dominance.

**Scoreboard vs reality:**
- North won contested ball 119-116 (marginal edge to North)
- North out-tackled West Coast 72-47 — significant work-rate advantage through the game
- BUT: West Coast had the momentum, the late ascendancy, and statistically the better Q4 (3 goals to 1)
- Waterman finished 3.3 — if even one of those three behinds converts, Eagles win comfortably

**Key factor — Jack Darling:** The ex-Eagle kicked four first-half goals for North Melbourne, which established the buffer. This was a particular psychological blow and explains why North's lead ballooned.

**Conclusion:** West Coast were genuinely the better team in Q4. They outplayed North late and the scoreline (one point) is deeply flattering to the Kangaroos. The margin should have been 5-6 points to West Coast.

**Market context:** West Coast were $2.62 outsiders vs North's $1.50. They nearly caused the upset and arguably deserved to.

## LUCKY TEAMS (won, but underlying stats tell a different story)

---

### SYDNEY SWANS — Lucky Winners (trailed by 9 at 3QT, escaped by 3)
**Result:** Won 93-90 vs Port Adelaide
**Why lucky:** Sydney were down nine points with one quarter to play in enemy territory. The final quarter comeback (5 goals to 3) is real, but the question is whether Sydney should have been down nine at three-quarter time against a team (4-9) that was 12th on the ladder.

**Scoreboard vs reality:**
- Port scored 25 total scoring shots, Sydney 23 — Port had MORE opportunities
- Sydney's accuracy in Q4 was stellar when it mattered most, but they needed Port to continue their poor shooting to hold them to 3 goals in Q4
- Chad Warner (26 disp, 1 goal, 10 disp / 3 clearances in Q4) was the legitimate match-winner — credit to him
- Isaac Heeney (30 disp, 1 goal) was busy throughout but the team was collectively below their best

**Lucky qualifier:** Sydney's record of 12-2 means they find ways to win even when not at their best. Whether this is "luck" or "class" is a genuine debate. But going down by 9 to a side with 4 wins suggests some fortune in the escape.

**Conclusion:** Partially lucky — Port's Q1-Q2 inaccuracy gifted Sydney the game. A better-shooting Port would have gone into Q4 with a 20-point lead that would have been unsurmountable.

---

### NORTH MELBOURNE — Lucky Winners (survived despite being out-played in Q4)
**Result:** Won 74-73 vs West Coast
**Why lucky:** North built a 20-point Q4 lead but then allowed West Coast to kick three unanswered goals. They were saved entirely by Waterman's miss. If that set shot from 30m goes through — which it should at AFL level — North loses.

**Scoreboard vs reality:**
- North won contested ball (119-116) and out-tackled West Coast (72-47) — suggests genuine merit in the win for three quarters
- West Coast's Q4 (3 goals to North's 1) was clearly superior
- Harry Sheezel (32 disp, 9 inside 50s, 9 clearances) was the engine all day
- Jack Darling's four first-half goals were the foundation — without those, North probably doesn't build the buffer

**Conclusion:** Merit-based win for three quarters, survived on a Waterman miss. The 1-point margin is accurate — it really was that close. North deserved the win through the game but were fortunate not to lose it in the final minute.

---

### BRISBANE LIONS — Lucky (half-time margin flattered West Coast's competition, not structural)
**Result:** Won 115-80 vs Richmond (by 35)
**Why partially lucky (but less so):** Brisbane led by just three points at half-time against a bottom-two Richmond side at a neutral venue (Hobart). For a reigning dual premier team, that's a concerning first half. The 35-point final margin flatters Brisbane significantly because the Q3 blitz (7 goals to 3) was built against a Richmond side that had run out of legs.

**Scoreboard vs reality:**
- Half-time margin: Brisbane +3 (115-80 final = 32-point Q3-Q4 blitz)
- Richmond's Jasper Alger kicked 4 goals for the Tigers — bottom-two teams should not be giving the reigning premiers this much trouble at half-time
- Brisbane's stars (Ashcroft 38 disp, Neale) were neutralised for a half, which is notable
- Eric Hipwood's 200th game return adds context — Brisbane were not at full fitness across the board

**Conclusion:** Mildly lucky that Richmond didn't push harder. The final margin (35 points) significantly overstates Brisbane's dominance — they were essentially level at half-time.

## GENUINELY DOMINANT WINNERS

---

### ADELAIDE CROWS — Dominant (57-point win was legitimate)
**Result:** Won 121-64 vs Western Bulldogs
**Stats:** 
- Q1: Adelaide 9 goals, Bulldogs 2 — inside 50 dominance from the first bounce
- Jordan Dawson 32 disposals, 3 goals; Sam Berry clearance engine; Riley Thilthorpe returned with impact
- The 57-point final margin actually understates Q1 dominance where Adelaide had built a 47-point lead

**Lucky qualifier:** Was there an element of the Bulldogs being off? Coming off wins, some pre-match assessments had Bulldogs ($1.95-$2.05) as slight favourites. The nine-goal Q1 requires noting: was this sustainable execution or Bulldogs switching off?
- Adelaide repeated the effort across Q2-Q4 and won those three quarters combined decisively too
- Verdict: **Genuinely dominant.** This was not a Q1 spike followed by regression — the Crows' control never slipped.

---

### MELBOURNE DEMONS — Dominant (45-point win reflected the stats)
**Result:** Won 95-50 vs Essendon
**Stats:**
- Inside 50s: Melbourne +22 (emphatic territorial control)
- Disposals: Melbourne +49
- Clearances: Melbourne +11
- Contested possessions: Melbourne +33
- Kysaiah Pickett: 32 disposals, 3 goals, 10 score involvements

**Lucky qualifier:** Essendon fought back in Q3, restricting Melbourne to one goal while kicking three themselves. So there was a competitive patch. But the overall statistics are so lopsided that the 45-point margin is, if anything, **flattering to Essendon** — the underlying numbers point to an even larger legitimate gap.

**Verdict:** Genuinely dominant. The stats in every key category are among the largest R14 differentials.

---

### GEELONG CATS — Dominant (but with Dangerfield management caveat)
**Result:** Won 105-60 vs Gold Coast
**Stats:**
- Jeremy Cameron 3 goals; seven-goal second quarter blitz
- Chris Scott post-match flagged Dangerfield rest for R15 (Perth trip) — this implies Dangerfield may not have played full output
- Gold Coast coach Hardwick: "execution not effort" — Gold Coast missed significant easy shots inside 50, kicking "some unbelievable goals" but lacking precision

**Lucky qualifier:** 
- Gold Coast's shooting accuracy was poor — Hardwick's "ridiculous" rule complaint suggests some bad luck for the Suns
- If Gold Coast kicks at average accuracy, the margin is probably 25-30 points, not 45
- Dangerfield being managed (came off the ground, flagged for R15 rest) suggests Cats weren't at 100%

**Verdict:** Genuinely dominant but the 45-point margin slightly inflated by Gold Coast inaccuracy. Geelong probably wins by 25-30 if Suns kick straight.
